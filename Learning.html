Feynman Technique

Step 1

Grab a sheet of paper and write the name of the concept at the top. You can use pretty much any concept or idea – even though the technique is named after Feynman, it’s not limited solely to math and science.

Step 2

Explain the concept in your own words as if you were teaching it to someone else. Focus on using plain, simple language. Don’t limit your explanation to a simple definition or a broad overview; challenge yourself to work through an example or two as well to ensure you can put the concept into action.

Step 3

Review your explanation and identify the areas where you didn’t know something or where you feel your explanation is shaky. Once you’ve pinpointed them, go back to the source material, your notes, or any examples you can find in order to shore up your understanding.

Step 4

If there are any areas in your explanation where you’ve used lots of technical terms or complex language, challenge yourself to re-write these sections in simpler terms. Make sure your explanation could be understood by someone without the knowledge base you believe you already have.

        • If you have a question or something comes to mind as you're taking notes, you have two choices: You can contribute to the class discussion by asking your question or making your comment. Or you can jot your question or comment down in your notes. [\[cse.buffalo.edu\]](https://www.cse.buffalo.edu//~rapaport/howtostudy.html)

      • 3.6. Copy Your Notes at Home [\[cse.buffalo.edu\]](https://www.cse.buffalo.edu//~rapaport/howtostudy.html)
        • Of course you should study your class notes at home; but just (re-)reading them is too passive.[\[cse.buffalo.edu\]](https://www.cse.buffalo.edu//~rapaport/howtostudy.html)
        • What I suggest is that you study your notes by re-writing them. For each class, buy a separate notebook from the one you take your notes in. I recommend a "composition" or spiral notebook, not a looseleaf notebook, for your "permanent" (i.e., re-written) notes. Then, as soon as possible after class (preferably that evening or the next), copy your notes into your permanent notebook.[\[cse.buffalo.edu\]](https://www.cse.buffalo.edu//~rapaport/howtostudy.html)
        • Personally, I like to number main ideas (and separate them with a line), using an "indented bullet" style for details:
        • 1\. Main idea 1 - detail 1 - detail 2 - further detail 2.1 - detail 3 - further detail 3.1 - further detail 3.2[\[cse.buffalo.edu\]](https://www.cse.buffalo.edu//~rapaport/howtostudy.html)
      • 3.7. Don't Take Notes on a Computer [\[cse.buffalo.edu\]](https://www.cse.buffalo.edu//~rapaport/howtostudy.html)
        • I do not recommend taking notes on a laptop computer during class. [\[cse.buffalo.edu\]](https://www.cse.buffalo.edu//~rapaport/howtostudy.html)
        • I find that I have a firmer grasp of what I write if I handwrite it than if I type it. (As Usama Fayyad has said: computers are "great at bookkeeping but not yet great at recording impromptu ideas, thoughts, feelings. For that, paper is still far superior. You can hold it, fold it, put it in your pocket, look at it again later when it's convenient" (as quoted in [Swerdlow 1999](https://www.cse.buffalo.edu//~rapaport/howtostudy.html#swerdlow): 130).) [\[cse.buffalo.edu\]](https://www.cse.buffalo.edu//~rapaport/howtostudy.html)

  • 5\. Read Texts Actively & Slowly, before & after Class [\[cse.buffalo.edu\]](https://www.cse.buffalo.edu//~rapaport/howtostudy.html)
    • 5.1. Read Actively, Not Passively [\[cse.buffalo.edu\]](https://www.cse.buffalo.edu//~rapaport/howtostudy.html)
    • 5.2. Read Slowly. [\[cse.buffalo.edu\]](https://www.cse.buffalo.edu//~rapaport/howtostudy.html)
      • "…an undeniable truth: that in the pursuit of knowledge, slower can be better." —Gleick, James (2011), The Information (New York: Pantheon): 404. [\[cse.buffalo.edu\]](https://www.cse.buffalo.edu//~rapaport/howtostudy.html)
    • 5.3. Highlight the Text in the Margin [\[cse.buffalo.edu\]](https://www.cse.buffalo.edu//~rapaport/howtostudy.html)
      • One, of course, is to highlight important or interesting passages. There are several ways to do this. The worst is to use a yellow highlighting marker (or hot pink, or whatever color you like). The main problem with this is that you will tend to find almost every sentence to be important or interesting.[\[cse.buffalo.edu\]](https://www.cse.buffalo.edu//~rapaport/howtostudy.html)
      • A slightly less messy, but equally useless, technique is to use a pen or pencil to underline important or interesting passages. I guarantee that you will wind up underlining every sentence on every page, and you will have gained nothing. [\[cse.buffalo.edu\]](https://www.cse.buffalo.edu//~rapaport/howtostudy.html)
      • I like to use the right margin and to make my line a right square bracket: \]. If you want to make it clear \[exactly where the highlighted passage begins or ends,\] [\[cse.buffalo.edu\]](https://www.cse.buffalo.edu//~rapaport/howtostudy.html)
      • Moreover, when you re-read the text (note that I said 'when', not 'if' :-), you can then use a different highlighting technique (e.g., underlining) to highlight more important passages. Sometimes, I use double brackets in the margin for this second round of highlighting: \]\] and underlining for a third round. [\[cse.buffalo.edu\]](https://www.cse.buffalo.edu//~rapaport/howtostudy.html)
    • 5.4. Make Notes in the Margin [\[cse.buffalo.edu\]](https://www.cse.buffalo.edu//~rapaport/howtostudy.html)
      • I like to put cross-references in the margin; e.g., if a passage on p. 20 reminds me in some way of a passage on p. 10, I'll write "see p. 10" in the margin on p. 20, and "see p. 20" in the margin on p. 10.[\[cse.buffalo.edu\]](https://www.cse.buffalo.edu//~rapaport/howtostudy.html)
      • But now suppose that a few months (or a few years) later, you want to find that interesting passage that related to, say, consciousness; how will you find it? You could, of course, page through the book till you find it, but what I like to do is to make an index of my marginal comments; you can add entries (e.g., Consciousness: 10, 20) to the book's index if it already has one, or use a blank page at the end of the book if it doesn't have an index. [\[cse.buffalo.edu\]](https://www.cse.buffalo.edu//~rapaport/howtostudy.html)
    • 5.5. Keep a Notebook [\[cse.buffalo.edu\]](https://www.cse.buffalo.edu//~rapaport/howtostudy.html)
      • The best technique for active reading is to keep a notebook. In addition to (or instead of) highlighting a passage, copy it—verbatim—into your notebook. [\[cse.buffalo.edu\]](https://www.cse.buffalo.edu//~rapaport/howtostudy.html)
      • Be sure to begin your notebook with a full [citation](https://www.cse.buffalo.edu//~rapaport/howtowrite.html#citations) to the text for use in a bibliography, and be sure to write down the page numbers of each passage that you copy. Then, write down—at length and in detail—your comments on the passage. (I sometimes like to use a pen for the text and a pencil for my commentary.) [\[cse.buffalo.edu\]](https://www.cse.buffalo.edu//~rapaport/howtostudy.html)
      • These notes can then be used later if you write a term paper or research paper that discusses the material in the text. For that purpose, it will be useful to number your notes. I find the following scheme useful: Number each notebook page with a [Roman numeral](http://www.google.com/search?q=%22roman+numerals%22) (I, II, etc.), number each quoted passage (or stand-alone comment) with an Arabic numeral (1, 2, etc.), and letter (a, b, etc.) each comment associated with a quoted passage (or stand-alone comment). Then you can refer to each passage with an identifier (like XIV-7-b, i.e., comment b about quotation 7, which comment is located on notebook page XIV) that will enable you to find it later. [See below](https://www.cse.buffalo.edu//~rapaport/howtostudy.html#makeoutline).) [\[cse.buffalo.edu\]](https://www.cse.buffalo.edu//~rapaport/howtostudy.html) this is briliant way to do research
    • 5.6. Read Literature Quickly and Passively the First Time. [\[cse.buffalo.edu\]](https://www.cse.buffalo.edu//~rapaport/howtostudy.html)
      • If the text is a work of literature (a story, novel, play, poem, etc.), it is often best to read it once all the way through without stopping, just as you would read something for fun, so that you get to know what it's about and can appreciate it as a work of literature. [\[cse.buffalo.edu\]](https://www.cse.buffalo.edu//~rapaport/howtostudy.html)
      • Actually, even for non-fiction, it can be useful to read the text through once, quickly, to get an overview, perhaps making notes if something strikes you, and then doing the slow and active reading techniques when you are studying the text. [\[cse.buffalo.edu\]](https://www.cse.buffalo.edu//~rapaport/howtostudy.html)
    • 5.7. Read Before and After Class [\[cse.buffalo.edu\]](https://www.cse.buffalo.edu//~rapaport/howtostudy.html)
      • Ideally, you should read a text at least twice. Read it (perhaps quickly) before the class in which it will be discussed, so that you are familiar with its contents. Then (re-)read it after class using the [slow and active method](https://www.cse.buffalo.edu//~rapaport/howtostudy.html#slow). If time permits, you can cut corners by only reading it—slowly and actively!—after class.[\[cse.buffalo.edu\]](https://www.cse.buffalo.edu//~rapaport/howtostudy.html)
  • 6\. Do Your Homework [\[cse.buffalo.edu\]](https://www.cse.buffalo.edu//~rapaport/howtostudy.html)
    • Science and math courses (and some others, such as foreign-language courses) often require you to do homework exercises or problem sets. I strongly recommend that you do not simply do the problems and hand them in. Rather, do them on scrap paper, check them over, and then copy them neatly. Turn in the neat copy (and, of course, be sure that your name is on it!). [\[cse.buffalo.edu\]](https://www.cse.buffalo.edu//~rapaport/howtostudy.html)
    • And don't just write down answers. Write down the problem and the complete solution showing how you arrived at your answer. [\[cse.buffalo.edu\]](https://www.cse.buffalo.edu//~rapaport/howtostudy.html)
  • 7\. Study for Exams [\[cse.buffalo.edu\]](https://www.cse.buffalo.edu//~rapaport/howtostudy.html)
    • 7.1. Don't Study for Exams! [\[cse.buffalo.edu\]](https://www.cse.buffalo.edu//~rapaport/howtostudy.html)
      • That's right: You shouldn't study only for exams. And you shouldn't study for the sake of exams.You should "study for learning and understanding": [\[cse.buffalo.edu\]](https://www.cse.buffalo.edu//~rapaport/howtostudy.html)
    • 7.2. Manage Your Time [\[cse.buffalo.edu\]](https://www.cse.buffalo.edu//~rapaport/howtostudy.html)
      • The first rule is: Don't cram! [\[cse.buffalo.edu\]](https://www.cse.buffalo.edu//~rapaport/howtostudy.html)
      • Do not be tempted, by any free time that you have during exam week, to do anything other than studying. (If you must take some time to relax, do it after you've done all your studying for the day.) If you have E exams and D days to study for them, spend roughly D/E days studying for each exam. (E.g., if you have 4 exams and 5 days to study for them, spend a little more than 1 day (1.25 days to be exact) studying for each exam.) [\[cse.buffalo.edu\]](https://www.cse.buffalo.edu//~rapaport/howtostudy.html) #practical-tips
      • Moreover, you should distribute your studying, not "mass" it all together: "set aside blocks of time throughout each week to study the content for each class"; "massed practive leads to quick learning and quick forgetting, whereas interleaved practice slows learning but leads to much greater retention"[\[cse.buffalo.edu\]](https://www.cse.buffalo.edu//~rapaport/howtostudy.html)
    • 7.3. How Not to Study [\[cse.buffalo.edu\]](https://www.cse.buffalo.edu//~rapaport/howtostudy.html)
      • re-reading your textbook has "little or no benefit" when you are studying for a test. [\[cse.buffalo.edu\]](https://www.cse.buffalo.edu//~rapaport/howtostudy.html)
      • you learn better and remember more from repeated testing (from both in-class quizzes and from self-testing at home) than from repeated reading [\[cse.buffalo.edu\]](https://www.cse.buffalo.edu//~rapaport/howtostudy.html)
    • 7.4. Make a Study Outline [\[cse.buffalo.edu\]](https://www.cse.buffalo.edu//~rapaport/howtostudy.html)
      • Use your recopied class notes, together with your highlighted text and notebook, to make an outline of the material. Try to put as much as possible onto the front sides of only 1 or 2 sheets of paper[\[cse.buffalo.edu\]](https://www.cse.buffalo.edu//~rapaport/howtostudy.html)
    • 7.5. Write Sample Essays & Do Sample Problems [\[cse.buffalo.edu\]](https://www.cse.buffalo.edu//~rapaport/howtostudy.html)
    • 7.6. Make "Flash Cards" [\[cse.buffalo.edu\]](https://www.cse.buffalo.edu//~rapaport/howtostudy.html)
      • For any subject, you can make a set of "flash cards". But I suggest using regular 8 1/2" x 11" paper, not index cards. Divide each page in half, vertically. On the left, write a "question" that requires an "answer", e.g., the name of a theorem, a term to be defined, the statement of a theorem, etc. [\[cse.buffalo.edu\]](https://www.cse.buffalo.edu//~rapaport/howtostudy.html)
      • Then memorize the questions and answers—but do not simply recite them by heart. Instead, write down the answers: Cover the right-hand side (the answers) with a blank sheet of paper, and write down the answers. When you finish a page, check your work and repeat writing the answers to the questions you missed until you get them all correct. [\[cse.buffalo.edu\]](https://www.cse.buffalo.edu//~rapaport/howtostudy.html)
      • Why write, and not merely recite? Because you will have to write the answers on the actual test; get used to writing them now. (Of course, if it's going to be an oral exam, reciting may be better than writing. Still, one tends to skip details when reciting, especially if you recite silently to yourself, but if you write the answers and have a good memory, then, during an oral exam, you can "read" the answers with your mind's eye.) [\[cse.buffalo.edu\]](https://www.cse.buffalo.edu//~rapaport/howtostudy.html)
    • 7.7. Stop Studying When You Feel Confident [\[cse.buffalo.edu\]](https://www.cse.buffalo.edu//~rapaport/howtostudy.html)
      • How do you know when you've studied enough? It's not when you're tired of studying! And it's not when you've gone through the material one time! You should stop only when you get to the point that you feel confident and ready for whatever will be on the exam—when you're actually eager to see the exam to find out if you guessed its contents correctly. [\[cse.buffalo.edu\]](https://www.cse.buffalo.edu//~rapaport/howtostudy.html)
  • 8\. Take Exams [\[cse.buffalo.edu\]](https://www.cse.buffalo.edu//~rapaport/howtostudy.html)
    • For an essay question, do a "mind dump": Write down, on scrap paper, brief reminders (keywords) of everything that you remember about the topic of the question. Then [develop an outline](https://www.cse.buffalo.edu//~rapaport/howtostudy.html#makeoutline) of your answer. Then write the essay. (With luck, much of the essay can be "copied from memory" from the sample essays you wrote when studying.) [\[cse.buffalo.edu\]](https://www.cse.buffalo.edu//~rapaport/howtostudy.html)
  • 9. Do Research & Write Essays. [\[cse.buffalo.edu\]](https://www.cse.buffalo.edu//~rapaport/howtostudy.html)
    • 9.1. Choose Topic Carefully [\[cse.buffalo.edu\]](https://www.cse.buffalo.edu//~rapaport/howtostudy.html)
      • Avoid the two extremes of a topic that is so broad or well-known that there are too many sources of information and a topic that is so narrow or little-known that there is a paucity of information.[\[cse.buffalo.edu\]](https://www.cse.buffalo.edu//~rapaport/howtostudy.html)
    • 9.3. Make an Outline [\[cse.buffalo.edu\]](https://www.cse.buffalo.edu//~rapaport/howtostudy.html)
      • This stage may require several iterations. [\[cse.buffalo.edu\]](https://www.cse.buffalo.edu//~rapaport/howtostudy.html)
      • How do you make an outline? [\[cse.buffalo.edu\]](https://www.cse.buffalo.edu//~rapaport/howtostudy.html)
      • First, write down a handful of main themes that you want to discuss (these will be the categories that you sorted your notes into); describe each using only a few keywords. Decide in what order you want to write about them, and then—on a blank piece of paper—put each at the head of a column, something like this:intro topic1 topic2 topic3 conclusion [\[cse.buffalo.edu\]](https://www.cse.buffalo.edu//~rapaport/howtostudy.html)
      • Next, in each column, write down the main ideas that you want to include, again ordering them and using just a few keywords. These will be your subsections. [\[cse.buffalo.edu\]](https://www.cse.buffalo.edu//~rapaport/howtostudy.html)
      • Under each of these, put the [identifying numbers](https://www.cse.buffalo.edu//~rapaport/howtostudy.html#ident) of the items in your notes that you want to include in each subsection. [\[cse.buffalo.edu\]](https://www.cse.buffalo.edu//~rapaport/howtostudy.html)
    • 9.4. Write, Using Your Outline [\[cse.buffalo.edu\]](https://www.cse.buffalo.edu//~rapaport/howtostudy.html)
      • Once you've got your outline, start writing, using your outline and notes as a guide. Don't spend too much time editing what you write at this stage. Just write. [\[cse.buffalo.edu\]](https://www.cse.buffalo.edu//~rapaport/howtostudy.html)
      • (I should note that some people prefer ["free writing"](http://wiki.ehow.com/Freewrite) , in which you don't spend any time preparing an outline before you write. If that works for you, go for it.) [\[cse.buffalo.edu\]](https://www.cse.buffalo.edu//~rapaport/howtostudy.html)
    • 9.5. Edit [\[cse.buffalo.edu\]](https://www.cse.buffalo.edu//~rapaport/howtostudy.html)
      • After you've written your first draft, re-read what you wrote, using the [method of slow and active reading](https://www.cse.buffalo.edu//~rapaport/howtostudy.html#readactively), and revise (or "edit") what you wrote. [\[cse.buffalo.edu\]](https://www.cse.buffalo.edu//~rapaport/howtostudy.html)
